# Climate-Data-Project
This study evaluates the predictive effectiveness of modern machine learning models against classical temporal models (SARIMA) on global temperatures. Bootstrap resampling and Forward-Chaining Cross-Validation will be utilized in constructing confidence intervals to assess model predictability and stability. 

The study will use the Goddard Institute for Space Studies Surface Temperature Analysis (GISTEMP v4) dataset developed by NASA. This dataset contains the averaged land-surface air and sea-surface water temperature anomalies (Land-Ocean Temperature Index, L-OTI), measured in Celsius relative to the 1951 – 1980 baseline. 

### Abstract
We evaluate the predictive performance and uncertainty of classical and modern machine learning models for global temperature anomaly fore-casting using NASA’s GISTEMP v4 dataset (1880–2025).  Particular attention is given to the challenges of temporal dependence and data leak-age in time series modeling, as well as the differences between model prediction and interpretability. To address these, we implement a forward-chaining cross-validation framework and a 10-year holdout evaluation, ensuring all models are assessed on identical chronological partitions. Five modeling approaches are compared: SARIMA as a classical baseline, Elastic Net regression, Random Forest, feed-forward neural networks using a sliding window architecture, and Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS). Model performance is evaluated using cross-validated mean squared error and holdout forecast accuracy, while bootstrap resampling is used to construct confidence intervals for pairwise performance dif-ferences. Results indicate that SARIMA provides a strong and stable baseline, particularly for short-term forecasting. BSTS achieves the best overall long-term performance, effectively capturing the upward trend in temperature anomalies while maintaining interpretability through latent component decomposition. Random Forest demonstrates competitive performance but fails to extrapolate beyond the training distribu-tion. Neural networks exhibit high variance and instability in long-horizon forecasts, while Elastic Net underperforms due to its limited ability to capture nonlinear dynamics and long-term trends. Model performance was also evaluated separately for the Northern and Southern Hemi-spheres within the context of BSTS, revealing differences consistent with data density and thermal dynamics. These findings highlight the importance of model structure and evaluation design in climate time series forecasting, and suggest that while machine learning methods offer flexibility and can scale with available computational resources, making them well-suited for capturing complex nonlinear dynamics in climate data, classical and semi-structured approaches such as SARIMA and BSTS remain highly competitive for this application, both in terms of short to medium-term prediction and in terms of model interpretability.
