# Climate-Data-Project
NASA climate anomaly data with ML and SARIMA. 

### Description:
In the recent century, we as a society have become increasingly aware of the ecological and environmental impacts we have had on our world. For instance, the Great Smog of London in 1952 was caused by coal-fired domestic heating and factory emissions, which combined with a local anticyclone and trapped the emissions produced over London for 5 days. An estimated 4,000 - 12,000 deaths are attributed to the smog. This event, and other such smogs in Dorona, Pennsylvania (1948), Los Angeles, California (1943), and St. Louis, Missouri (1939), eventually brought about the Clean Air Act of 1970. In the late 1970s, scientists led by Dr. James Hansen at Goddard Institute of Space Studies (GISS) became concerned with greenhouse gases having a major impact on global surface temperatures. Dr. Hansen and his team noted that previous attempts to study the changes in the global surface temperature were limited to the Northern Hemisphere. Their efforts to correct this later led to the GISTemp Analysis of the Global Temperature, and the agreed upon Global Baseline: an average of the global temperature (based on over 20,000 data points) from the years 1951 - 1980 (chosen to align with the NWS three decade ‘normal’). This was set at 14°C or 47°F, which will usually be referenced as 0. Building on Dr. Hansen’s work, we have a dataset for observing and predicting anomalies in global temperature changes. We will use this robust data to develop machine learning methods that model global temperature anomalies and make predictions about future deviations from the baseline. We will assess each model’s reliability in the Northern and Southern Hemisphere and examine how the models differ for these regions. Although Dr. Hansen’s research increased our knowledge of the Southern Hemisphere, the available data for this region remains much sparser than that of the Northern Hemisphere, and may require model adjustments to accurately represent it. As an extension we may discuss changes in data collection, specifically the decrease in data points after 1998 due to the urban heat island (UHI) effect, which can lead to an overestimation of overall temperature anomalies. Further extensions could include developing models for regional temperature changes.

### Objective 
This study evaluates the predictive effectiveness of modern machine learning models against classical SARIMA time series models for predicting global temperatures in both the Northern
and Southern Hemisphere. This study will use NASA’s GISTEMP v4 dataset and apply machine learning methods, employing bootstrap resampling to construct confidence intervals and k-fold cross-validation for parameter tuning. To assess the relative performance of each method, we will apply bootstrap resampling to the paired differences in cross-validated prediction errors between each candidate model and the SARIMA baseline, yielding confidence intervals for performance differences. This framework enables statistically meaningful comparison of predictive accuracy and uncertainty, while accounting for temporal dependence and differences in data availability between hemispheres.

### Statistical Methods
The predictive models used to estimate global and hemispheric temperature anomalies are described below, along with the procedures used to evaluate their predictive accuracy, uncertainty, and generalization performance. All models will be evaluated using a common, time-series-aware k-fold cross-validation scheme, ensuring that each method is trained and validated on identical chronological data partitions. This approach enables fair, paired comparison of prediction errors across modeling approaches. Predictors will, at minimum include, time index variables such as month and year, seasonal indicators, and time-lag variables to account for temporal dependence and autocorrelation. To quantify uncertainty in relative model performance, bootstrap resampling will be applied to the cross-validated error differences between each candidate model and the SARIMA baseline. This approach yields confidence intervals for performance differences and accounts for variability across the timeframes used for all models. The project adopts a comparative modeling framework in which multiple statistical and machine learning approaches are evaluated side by side, enabling a systematic comparison of predictive accuracy, uncertainty behavior, and hemispheric robustness, given the differences in data availability between the Northern and Southern hemispheres. Multiple Linear Regression Baseline model for trend-based climate prediction. Can work as a reference for more complex methods. Ordinary Least Squares (OLS); indexing by time and seasonal indicators (month, year, or season). Allows comparison with more complex models against a simple, interpretable standard. Polynomial Regression Captures nonlinear long-term warming trends that can’t be modeled by linear terms alone. OLS or regularized estimation. Improves trend representation while remaining interpretable. Helps assess whether nonlinear temporal effects improve predictive accuracy. Lasso Regression Performs feature selection in the presence of correlated temporal features. Regularization; sparse coefficient estimation. Identifies the most influential temporal predictors more easily while avoiding overfitting. Random Forest Regression Captures the nonlinear interactions expected in time series climate data and complex temporal patterns without requiring explicit parameterizations or assumptions. Ensemble of decision trees; bootstrap aggregation (bagging). With minimal assumptions, improves predictive performance for complex relationships, including patterns that may be difficult to discern without overfitting when using high-degree polynomials. Provides robustness to noise, supporting the objective of reducing MSE. Neural Network Models nonlinear temporal dynamics and interactions that are not tractable using traditional regression approaches. Multilayer perceptron. Evaluates whether flexible nonlinear models improve long-term prediction and uncertainty quantification relative to simpler methods. SARIMA (Baseline Comparator) Provides a traditional time-series benchmark that is often used for this kind of climate modeling. Seasonal ARIMA; autoregressive and moving average terms. Serves as a reference model to evaluate whether machine learning methods yield lower MSE and improved uncertainty handling.
